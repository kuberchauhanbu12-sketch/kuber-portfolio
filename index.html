Case Study 01 · B2B SaaS · Strategic Product Pivot
The Signal in the Noise:
Engineering Commitment
How I diagnosed why HireBound's 64% response rate still couldn't prevent 30–50% early attrition — and redesigned the entire qualification architecture to fix the right problem.
Company Type
B2B Hiring SaaS
Core Problem
30–50% early attrition
Market Opportunity
4–5 Lakh roles/month
Target Outcome
25% → 40–45% intent rate
TL;DR HireBound had a 64% response rate but 30–50% early attrition. I found the funnel was measuring the wrong thing entirely. I redesigned it with a 3-stage Intent & Fit Qualification Layer that runs before skill matching — projecting high-intent pipeline from 25% → 40–45% with a guardrail to protect top-of-funnel volume.
The Hook — What Everyone Was Getting Wrong

When I first looked at HireBound's numbers, everything looked fine. A ~64% top-of-funnel response rate is genuinely impressive for a hiring platform. Most product teams would call that a win and ship it.

But I noticed something that changed my entire framing: the response rate was high, yet clients were churning. Recruiters were complaining. And 30–50% of placed candidates were leaving within 90 days. The product was technically working — and completely failing at the same time.

That's when I asked the question nobody had asked yet: What is a 64% response rate actually measuring?

"A system can be highly active and completely unreliable at the same time. HireBound had optimised for the wrong signal."
Context — Why This Market Is Uniquely Hard

India fills 4–5 lakh sales roles every single month. That's not a niche problem — it's a massive, high-repeat hiring market. And high repeat demand means even a marginal improvement in candidate quality and retention unlocks compounding business value for every client.

But here's what makes sales hiring brutal: it has some of the highest early attrition rates across any industry. 30–50% of candidates leave within 90 days. Not because they couldn't do the job — but because of misaligned expectations, overpromised roles, and candidates who were never genuinely committed to begin with.

HireBound's opportunity wasn't to fix a broken product. It was to pivot the entire value proposition from "we find you candidates" to "we find you candidates who stay."

4–5L

Diagnosing the Real Problem — Not the Symptom

Most people looking at this data would say the problem is candidate quality. I didn't think that was right. I believed the problem was upstream — in the system's design itself.

I mapped the existing funnel and found something critical: the current architecture goes directly from a massive candidate pool → skill-based qualification → pipeline. It never asks whether candidates are actively looking, whether they can commit to the job's actual demands, or whether their expectations match reality.

The result: false positives at scale. Candidates who look qualified on paper, respond because the friction is low, but were never truly committed. The funnel is optimised for speed of matching, not reliability of hiring.

The Flawed Funnel — Symptom · Diagnosis · Result
Symptom
Candidates are marked as strong matches prematurely.
Diagnosis
Skill-based qualification bypasses expectation alignment entirely.
Result
Optimised for speed of matching, not reliability of hiring.
"Hiring is not a matching problem. It is a commitment prediction problem."
My Approach — Understanding Both Sides of the Marketplace

Before designing anything, I needed to understand the real pain on both sides. I built a Marketplace Friction Matrix to map the goals, failures, and fears of both users — not just the recruiter, who was the obvious customer.

The current system optimises for response volume — creating high activity but low mutual trust on both sides.

This exercise gave me the real insight: both users are being failed by the same root cause — a system that treats hiring as a matching game instead of a trust and commitment problem. Sarah needs reliability. Manish needs transparency. Neither is getting it.

The Solution — The Intent & Fit Qualification Layer

My solution was to add a lightweight pre-qualification system that runs before skill matching. The key design constraint I set for myself: it had to be fast enough that candidates don't drop off, but thorough enough to genuinely filter for commitment.

I designed 3 stages in sequence, each with a specific job to do:

But I wanted to go further. I noticed that even this layer had a friction problem: every new interaction would re-ask the same questions. So I designed a second layer — the Candidate Memory Layer.

What I Deliberately Chose Not To Do — And Why

This is where I think most case studies go wrong — they only show the solution. Here's what I rejected and the reasoning behind each decision:

❌ Rejected Option
Add more skill-assessment questions at top of funnel
✓ Why I Rejected It
Skills aren't the problem. A more skilled candidate with low intent still leaves. This would increase drop-off and not improve retention.
❌ Rejected Option
Build a long multi-step screening form
✓ Why I Rejected It
High-friction screening would hurt top-of-funnel. The system's response rate advantage is an asset. I designed stages to take under 45 seconds total.
❌ Rejected Option
Replace the existing skill qualification system
✓ Why I Rejected It
The existing system works. I designed my layer to precede it, not replace it — minimising engineering risk while maximising impact.
Success Metrics — How I'd Know If This Actually Worked

I defined three tiers of metrics — primary, secondary, and guardrail — because a good PM doesn't just measure the thing they optimised for. They also watch for what could go wrong.

Primary Metric

High-intent response rate. This is the core proof the system is filtering for commitment, not volume.
Secondary Metrics
↑ 90-day retention rate
↑ Interview conversion rate
↑ Client repeat rate
↓ Time to hire
Guardrail Metric
Top-of-funnel response rate must NOT drop below 50%. If the pre-qualification adds friction that kills responses, the cure is worse than the disease.
The Takeaway — What This Case Study Shows About How I Think
"The best hiring systems don't just find candidates — they find candidates who stay."

What I'm most proud of in this case study isn't the solution itself — it's the reframing of the problem. Everyone looking at HireBound saw a response rate. I saw a retention problem disguised as a volume win.

The skill I'm developing as a PM isn't just designing solutions. It's knowing which problem is actually worth solving — and having the discipline to reject the obvious answer when it's the wrong one.

Case Study 02 · Consumer App · EdTech · Retention
The Guided Choice Strategy:
Fixing Duolingo's Habit Death Zone
How I diagnosed why Duolingo's most engaged segment drops from 100% to 15% retention by Day 7 — and designed a choice architecture that repairs the habit loop without breaking the learning system.
Product
Duolingo (EdTech)
Core Problem
Day 7 Retention Drop
Target Segment
18–24 year-olds
Target Outcome
+20% Day 7 Retention
TL;DR Duolingo's greatest strength — structured learning — was actively causing early churn. I identified the core conflict as an autonomy-structure imbalance, then designed a Guided Choice Layer giving users 3–4 session options on each login during Days 1–7. Validated via 50/50 A/B test targeting +20% Day 7 retention with a guardrail to protect long-term learning quality.
The Hook — A Product That Fights Its Own Users

When I mapped Duolingo's Day 7 retention curve for the 18–24 segment, the pattern was stark. Users arrive with genuine intent — they set a language goal, they opened the app, they started. By Day 2 retention is ~40%. By Day 7 it's ~15%.

The instinct is to say "users aren't motivated enough." I rejected that entirely. These users were motivated enough to download, set up, and start. The problem had to be inside the product. So I asked the uncomfortable question: What is Duolingo doing during Days 1–7 that is actively making committed users leave?

"Our greatest strength — structured learning — is causing our greatest weakness — early churn."
Why Day 7 Is the Most Valuable Number in Duolingo's Business

Day 7 retention is not just a health metric. It is the absolute prerequisite for LTV in a freemium model. The chain is: Early Habit (Days 1–7) → Day 7 Retention → Long-term Daily Engagement → Subscription Conversion (5–8%) + Maximised Ad Impressions. Every Day 7 loss is a permanently lost revenue opportunity at Duolingo's scale.

~15%
Retention remaining by Day 7 in 18–24 segment
5–8%
Free-to-paid conversion for retained habit-forming users
10–15m
Avg daily session — short enough to fix the habit, long enough to matter
Diagnosing the Habit Death Zone — The System vs. The User

I built a Diagnostic Matrix mapping what the system does vs. how the user experiences it. This is where the root cause became clear.

System Reality
User Feeling
Fixed, strict linear learning path.
Lack of control and autonomy.
Highly predictable early lessons.
Repetitive and monotonous early experience.
Competitive elements locked behind progression.
Work before play — delayed time to fun.
Weak trigger-action-reward cycle.
Inconsistent motivation to return.
The system is actively burning out early motivation by enforcing a one-size-fits-all progression sequence.
The Core Conflict — Autonomy vs. Structure

Total Chaos (complete autonomy) = high motivation, low educational effectiveness. Rigid Dictation (current state) = high educational effectiveness, high churn. The solution must live exactly in the middle: Guided Choice — freedom within boundaries. The design challenge was injecting agency without sacrificing the learning outcomes that make Duolingo actually work.

Total Chaos
High motivation, low learning effectiveness
★ Guided Choice
Freedom within boundaries — the optimal synthesis
Rigid Dictation
Current state. High effectiveness, high churn.
The Solution — The Guided Choice Layer

On each login during Days 1–7: instead of a forced lesson, the user sees a Choice Screen capped at 3–4 options. Every path converges to the same destination — XP earned and streak maintained — but the user chose their path. That single act of choice transforms obligation into commitment.

⚡
Quick Challenge
High-energy bursts + competitive elements. Available from Day 1. Eliminates the "work before play" problem immediately — game fun no longer locked behind 5 lessons.
📚
Practice Basics
The core structured programme — same content, but now chosen voluntarily. Transforms a forced routine into a deliberate daily commitment.
🎧
Listening Mode
Reduces visual cognitive load. Built for commuting. Keeps the streak alive on low-energy days without forcing a full session or breaking the habit chain.
🛡️
Streak Saver
Low-friction safety net. Frequency-capped and difficulty-dynamically-increasing — prevents users from relying on it daily, which would undermine learning quality.
What I Deliberately Did Not Do — And Why
❌ Rejected
Give users unlimited freedom over content
✓ Why Not
Complete autonomy destroys educational outcomes. Users would skip hard lessons forever. The constraint is the product's core value.
❌ Rejected
Apply Guided Choice beyond Day 7
✓ Why Not
Day 7 is the habit formation window. Once users are retained, structured progression resumes. Extending choice indefinitely degrades long-term learning quality.
❌ Rejected
More than 4 options per Choice Screen
✓ Why Not
Decision fatigue. The paradox of choice means more options = less action = kills the goal entirely. Hard cap is a design feature, not a limitation.
Experiment Design & Three-Tier Metrics

50/50 A/B split. Group A: current linear experience. Group B: choice-based experience. Duration: 2–4 weeks. Constraint: leverage existing gamification infrastructure only — no aggressive paid conversion pushes during the test window.

Primary
+20%
Day 7 Retention — the single most critical number.
Secondary
↑ Session completion rate
↑ Streak continuation rate
↑ Average session time
↑ DAU to MAU ratio
Guardrail
Core learning progression must not decline. If Streak Saver becomes the dominant choice, retention is up but the product fails. Monitor Streak Saver usage rate carefully.
The Takeaway
"By blending structured progression with perceived autonomy, we transform a fleeting curiosity into a permanent habit."

What I'm most proud of isn't designing four mode options. It's identifying exactly where in the user journey agency needed to be injected — the daily login moment — and understanding that the architecture had to feel free while still converging on the same destination.

The guardrail metric was actually the hardest to design. It's easy to improve retention by making everything easier. The challenge was improving retention without destroying what makes Duolingo actually work.

Case Study 03 · Two-sided Marketplace · Mobility
Optimising Uber Night Service:
Saving 39,000 Rides Every Night
How I found that Uber's night cancellation crisis wasn't a driver problem or a rider problem — it was an information asymmetry problem affecting both sides simultaneously — and built a 2-pillar framework to fix it.
Focus Window
18:00 – 05:00
Baseline
25% cancellation rate
Target
17.5% in 6 months
Rides Recovered
39,000 per night
TL;DR Uber loses 130,000 rides every night in India to cancellations. The root cause wasn't driver behaviour — it was information asymmetry on both sides. My 2-pillar framework closes the driver blind spot (supply) and the rider blind spot (demand), projecting 39,000 recovered rides/night — a 30% reduction in 6 months.
The Hook — Why Cancellations Are Worse Than They Look

A 25% night cancellation rate sounds like an operational problem you fix with a policy change. When I mapped the full system, I realised it was far more structural. Night cancellations don't just lose one transaction — they trigger a negative feedback doom loop that damages the entire marketplace.

Cancelled ride → Driver wasted fuel and time on an unfulfilled pickup → Rider waits longer, grows anxious → Rider switches to a competitor app → Platform loses a user → Revenue drops → Fewer drivers incentivised to work nights → More cancellations next night. Every cancellation makes the next one more likely.

Sizing the Opportunity
1.3M
Total daily rides in India
520K
Night rides (40% of total)
130K
Night cancellations daily
39K
Rides to recover per night
My Approach — Mapping Both Sides Before Forming Any Hypothesis

The most dangerous move in a two-sided marketplace problem is blaming one side too early. I forced myself to deeply understand both before forming any solution hypothesis.

Demand Side — Riders
Age 18–30
Price-sensitive, actively compares apps. Highly prone to switching if delayed even slightly at night.
Age 31–60
Values reliability above all. Less likely to switch — but once trust breaks at night, they may never return.
Core Need
Fast confirmation, transparency, safety, and reliable pickup when they're most vulnerable.
Supply Side — Drivers
New (0–2 years)
Building ratings. Incentive-motivated. Generally accepts most rides to establish reputation.
Experienced (3–5 years)
Highly selective. Ruthlessly avoids uncertainty. Prioritises familiar, profitable routes only.
Core Need
Profitable rides, predictable routes, safety, and clear earnings visibility before committing.
Strategic conclusion: Drivers hold ultimate veto power — they accept, ignore, or cancel every ride. Supply-side confidence must be the primary focus.
Root Cause — Information Asymmetry on Both Sides

After mapping both sides, the real pattern became visible. Neither side was behaving irrationally. Both were making perfectly rational decisions based on incomplete information. The cancellations weren't a motivation problem. They were an information problem.

"Most cancellations occur because neither side has the full picture. Uncertainty breeds cancellations."
🚗 Driver Blind Spots (cause driver cancellations)
→Exact drop location and destination before accepting
→Route convenience and live traffic conditions
→Immediate trip profitability per kilometre
🙋 Rider Blind Spots (cause rider abandonment)
→Realistic expected wait time for this specific booking
→True ride confirmation status in real-time
→Actual driver commitment to the pickup
The 2-Pillar Solution — Closing Both Blind Spots Simultaneously

I designed two parallel pillars. They work together: Pillar 1 removes driver uncertainty (supply). Pillar 2 removes rider anxiety (demand). Neither alone is sufficient.

🔵 Pillar 1 — Empowering Supply (Drivers)
A: Directional Drop-offs
Show direction + distance range (e.g. "Northwest, 8–12km") before acceptance — not the exact address. Cures route anxiety without compromising rider privacy.
B: Night Surge Incentives
Targeted bonus payouts during 18:00–05:00. Compensates for lower volume and higher perceived risk — fundamentally shifts driver motivation to accept night requests.
🟠 Pillar 2 — Reassuring Demand (Riders)
C: Accurate Wait Timers
Real-time, accurate arrival clocks proactively reduce rider anxiety and prevent app-switching before it happens.
D: Smart Reassignment
If driver rejects, system instantly triggers background re-routing. The friction is masked from the rider — they never know a rejection happened.
E: Booking Transparency
Clear UI states: Searching → Accepted → Arriving. Keeps the rider anchored to the platform and builds confidence in the process at night.
What I Chose Not To Do — The Hard Trade-offs
❌ Rejected
Show full destination upfront to all drivers
✓ Why Not
Experienced drivers would cherry-pick only long, high-value routes — leaving short-trip riders stranded and reducing overall marketplace efficiency.
❌ Rejected
Penalise drivers financially for cancellations
✓ Why Not
Punitive measures reduce night driver supply, making the original problem worse. Drivers cancel because of uncertainty, not laziness. Fix the uncertainty.
❌ Rejected
Focus exclusively on financial incentives
✓ Why Not
Surge pricing alone doesn't fix route anxiety. A driver who doesn't know where they're going cancels even with a bonus. Both pillars must work in parallel.
Three-Tier Success Metrics
Primary
25%→17.5%
Night Cancellation Rate — the single headline number.
Secondary
↑ Driver acceptance rate
↑ Ride completion rate
↑ Night active users
↑ Revenue per night hour
Guardrail
Incentive cost must not exceed revenue recovered. Monitor cost-per-recovered-ride against incremental revenue. If surge costs more than it saves, the economics fail.
The Takeaway
"The best marketplace solutions don't pick sides. They give each side exactly the information they need to make the right decision."

The most important decision in this case study was refusing to pick a side. Every PM's instinct when seeing a cancellation problem is to focus on drivers or riders. The insight — that both sides were rational actors constrained by missing information — is what made a real solution possible.

The guardrail metric — monitoring incentive cost against recovered revenue — is what separates a product decision from a financial disaster. Good product thinking always includes the question: "What could make this go badly even if it seems to work?"
